# Politics & Governance Prediction Rubric

When researching political prediction markets, systematically evaluate these 12 dimensions. Prioritize higher-weighted dimensions but cover all of them.

## Dimensions

### 1. Resolution Criteria Parsing (weight: 0.15)
Carefully read the market's resolution rules. Identify exactly what must happen for YES vs NO. Common pitfalls: confusing "will win" (election day) vs "will be inaugurated" (takes office), or missing conditional clauses ("if the election is held before X").

### 2. Quantitative Base Rates (weight: 0.12)
Find historical frequencies for similar situations. Examples: How often do incumbents win re-election? How often do frontrunners in polls at this stage go on to win? What's the base rate for this type of political event? Always cite the specific statistic and source.

### 3. Polling & Public Sentiment (weight: 0.12)
Gather the most recent polling data. Evaluate poll quality: sample size, methodology (likely voters vs registered voters vs adults), pollster rating/track record, recency. Weight high-quality polls more heavily. Note polling averages vs individual polls.

### 4. Institutional & Procedural Constraints (weight: 0.10)
Account for legal, constitutional, and procedural barriers. Examples: Can the candidate actually run (age, citizenship, term limits)? What legislative process is required? Are there veto points, supermajority requirements, or judicial review risks?

### 5. Timeline Feasibility (weight: 0.10)
Is the predicted outcome feasible given the remaining time? Consider: campaign filing deadlines, legislative calendars, judicial timelines, bureaucratic processes. A probability estimate must be consistent with what can realistically happen before the resolution date.

### 6. Primary Institutional Sourcing (weight: 0.08)
Prioritize primary sources: official government records, court filings, election commission data, legislative transcripts, verified official statements. Deprioritize: opinion pieces, social media speculation, unnamed sources, partisan media spin.

### 7. Rhetoric-vs-Execution Disambiguation (weight: 0.08)
Distinguish between what politicians say and what they can/will do. Campaign promises, executive orders, and legislative proposals have very different likelihoods of execution. Consider: political will, institutional constraints, coalition dynamics, historical follow-through rates.

### 8. Temporal Discipline (weight: 0.07)
Distinguish current state from future trajectory. A candidate leading now may lose momentum. A bill with current support may face opposition in committee. Explicitly model how the situation might evolve between now and resolution, rather than anchoring on today's snapshot.

### 9. Geospatial Precision (weight: 0.05)
For location-specific markets, correctly scope the geography. National polls don't predict state-level outcomes. City politics differ from state politics. International markets require country-specific institutional knowledge. Avoid applying one region's patterns to another.

### 10. Counterfactual Reasoning (weight: 0.05)
Identify the 2-3 most impactful events that would change your estimate. For each: what is it, how likely is it, and how much would it shift the probability? This becomes the fragility assessment.

### 11. Source Diversity (weight: 0.04)
Use at least 3 independent sources. Cross-reference claims. If all evidence comes from one political perspective or one media ecosystem, actively seek opposing viewpoints. Consensus across diverse sources increases confidence.

### 12. Calibration Awareness (weight: 0.04)
Avoid probability estimates that cluster at 50% (pure uncertainty) or at extremes (99%/1%) without strong justification. Well-calibrated predictions use the full probability range. Ask: am I defaulting to 50% because I'm uncertain, or because the evidence genuinely points there?
