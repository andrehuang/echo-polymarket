# Economics & Finance Prediction Rubric

When researching economics/finance prediction markets, systematically evaluate these 12 dimensions.

## Dimensions

### 1. Resolution Criteria Parsing (weight: 0.15)
Read resolution rules precisely. Common pitfalls: "Fed cuts rates" may mean 25bps vs any cut vs specific target range; "recession" may use NBER definition vs two consecutive quarters of negative GDP; specific data releases use preliminary vs revised numbers.

### 2. Quantitative Base Rates (weight: 0.12)
Historical frequencies are essential. Examples: How often has the Fed cut in similar economic conditions? What's the base rate for recession given current leading indicators? How often do initial rate decisions get reversed within N months?

### 3. Leading Indicator Analysis (weight: 0.12)
Gather current economic data: employment figures, inflation readings (CPI/PCE), PMI/ISM surveys, yield curve shape, consumer confidence, housing starts, initial claims. Weight leading indicators more than lagging ones.

### 4. Central Bank Communication (weight: 0.10)
Parse Fed/ECB/BOJ communications: dot plots, meeting minutes, speeches by officials, forward guidance language. "Data dependent" vs specific thresholds. Track shifts in tone across recent statements. Fed funds futures pricing as market expectation.

### 5. Market Pricing & Expectations (weight: 0.10)
Check: Fed funds futures probabilities (CME FedWatch), Treasury yield curve, breakeven inflation rates, credit spreads, VIX. These embed thousands of sophisticated participants' views and are hard to beat systematically.

### 6. Policy & Fiscal Context (weight: 0.08)
Government spending, tax policy changes, trade policy (tariffs), sanctions, industrial policy. These affect economic outcomes but are often underweighted vs monetary policy. Consider: stimulus/austerity, debt ceiling risks, government shutdown probability.

### 7. Timeline Feasibility (weight: 0.08)
Economic processes have lag times. Monetary policy affects the economy with 6-18 month lags. Fiscal policy may be faster but still has implementation delays. A rate cut today won't prevent next quarter's recession if the data already shows contraction.

### 8. Geopolitical Risk Assessment (weight: 0.07)
Supply chain disruptions, energy price shocks, trade wars, military conflicts — these can rapidly change economic trajectories. Assess current geopolitical risks and their potential economic transmission channels.

### 9. Temporal Discipline (weight: 0.06)
Distinguish: current economic state vs trajectory. The economy can be strong now but weakening, or weak but recovering. Nowcasting models (Atlanta Fed GDPNow, NY Fed) provide real-time estimates distinct from forecasts.

### 10. Counterfactual Reasoning (weight: 0.05)
Identify 2-3 events that would shift your estimate: unexpected data prints (hot CPI, jobs miss), geopolitical shock, financial stress event (bank failure), policy surprise (emergency meeting).

### 11. Source Diversity (weight: 0.04)
Use: official data sources (BLS, BEA, Fed), market data (Bloomberg, FRED), forecaster consensus (Survey of Professional Forecasters, Blue Chip), model-based estimates (GDPNow). Avoid relying on any single forecaster or pundit.

### 12. Calibration Awareness (weight: 0.03)
Economic forecasting has a poor track record at turning points. Consensus forecasts rarely predict recessions in advance. Be appropriately humble about tail risks. If your estimate differs significantly from market pricing, examine why carefully — you need a specific informational edge.
